Students must be allowed to challenge their professors’ ideals

Read Time : 2 minutes
June 20th, 2019

Manickam Valliappan Shunning debate and free speech only discourages rational exchange of thoughts between indivduals.

Let’s face it. Relative to the west, Indian college students hardly have the freedom to dispute conventional ideas. Thoughts that are considered “taboo” such as sexual freedom and marijuana legislation hardly see the light of the day. But this is not just a problem with the good ol’ curriculum. A large part of it has it got to do with those who run it: professors.

This is not to say that all professors shun free thought and refuse to debate their students on polarising ideas. Some of them are liberal and allow such discourses. But a large chunk of India’s academia tend to hold traditionalist thought, and sadly, the firm condescension that their ideologies are right.

I still remember the time when I tried to have a discussion on marijuana legalisation with a professor. Throughout the conversation, all I could hear was “It’s just wrong” and his various misinterpretations of my arguments. “It helps cancer” was somehow translated to “It cures cancer”, “non-addictive psychoactive experience” was interpreted as “a serious problem of drug addiction”. As long as he could conclude with an “It’s just wrong”, the basis of interpretation was of no importance to him.

When the “debate” got to a tense point and his condescension had taken over his rationality, he did what most Indian professors would do when challenged: he kicked me out.

I do not mean to imply that I have won the debate or it has been concluded that marijuana must be legalised. But this is a perfect example of academicians — who are supposed to be the very beacons of free debate — stifling students’ free speech in order to uphold their traditional beliefs.

This incident is just the tip of the iceberg. On the institutional level, things get even dirtier. In various institutions throughout the nation, LGBTQ awareness events; screenings on menstruation and late caste activists; and many other campaigns have been shut down by administrations with no reason or on the irrational basis of “unnecessary”.

Most people assume that the role of institutions stops with helping students in his/her academic progress. But it extends beyond that. Universities also have the duty to mould responsible citizens of the country — and a vital ability of a responsible citizen is the ability to dissent.

Forcing students to adhere to a set of conventions and norms, and stripping them of their right to expression, only fosters them as submissive individuals who are hardly encouraged to dispute pressing issues of the Indian polity. Universities, and therefore the professors in it, must take an active role in encouraging students to challenge firmly held ideals so that more rational, prudent, and logical views can replace conservative and bigoted perspectives held by a society.

If it is felt that a student’s dissenting opinion doesn’t have enough logical backing, it is the professors duty to rationally justify why the student’s argument doesn’t make sense, instead of shutting down the debate all together. Shunning debate would just be a blatant act of intellectual cowardice, and discourages rational exchange of thoughts between individuals.

Universities must be places where cultural debate and change must be ignited, not shut down. If students aren’t allowed to dispute societal conventions at universities — which are supposed to be the very sanctities of free speech and expression — then what exactly is the point of its existence? Rote learning?
